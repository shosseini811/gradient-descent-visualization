How Gradient Descent Works for \(f(x) = x^4 - 4x^2 + 5\)

Gradient descent is an optimization algorithm used to minimize a function by iteratively moving in the direction of steepest descent as defined by the negative of the gradient (derivative).

In this visualization:

  • The Blue Curve: Represents our cost function \(f(x) = x^4 - 4x^2 + 5\)
  • The Red Dot: Represents our current position on the curve
  • The Green Path: Shows how gradient descent moves toward the minimum
  • The Purple Lines: Show the tangent lines at each point
  • The Red Arrows: Show the negative tangent direction (downhill direction)

Mathematical Properties:

Function: \[ f(x) = x^4 - 4x^2 + 5 \]

Derivative: \[ f'(x) = 4x^3 - 8x \]

Critical Points (where \(f'(x) = 0\)):

  • \(x = 0\) (local maximum)
  • \(x = -\sqrt{2}\) (local minimum)
  • \(x = \sqrt{2}\) (local minimum)

Convergence: Which minimum the algorithm finds depends on the starting position

Direction: In this visualization, we follow the negative tangent direction (downhill) rather than the perpendicular gradient direction

Learning Rate (\(\alpha\)):

Controls how big of a step we take in the direction of the negative gradient. A higher learning rate means bigger steps.

\[ x_{t+1} = x_t - \alpha \cdot f'(x_t) \]

Iterations:

The maximum number of steps the algorithm will take.
